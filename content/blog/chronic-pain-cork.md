Persistent pain can affect work, sleep, relationships, movement, and confidence. If you are looking for chronic pain support in Cork, the most useful starting point is to understand what a service offers, what it does not offer, and whether it fits alongside your existing medical care.

This article provides general educational information. It is not a diagnosis and does not replace assessment or treatment from your GP, consultant, physiotherapist, or other regulated healthcare professional.

## What Does Chronic Pain Mean?

Chronic or persistent pain is commonly used to describe pain that continues for more than three months. There are many possible reasons for pain to persist, and those reasons cannot be determined from an article or questionnaire.

Pain is real whether or not a scan fully explains its intensity. For some people, sensitisation of the nervous system may be one part of the picture. That possibility should be considered in the context of the person's symptoms, medical history, and appropriate clinical assessment.

Seek prompt medical advice for pain that is new, severe, changing, unexplained, associated with new neurological symptoms, or otherwise concerning.

## What Brain-Body Support May Explore

Brain-body and biopsychosocial approaches look at how biological, psychological, and social factors can interact with a person's pain experience. An educational session may explore:

-   the history and pattern of the symptoms
-   what medical assessment has already taken place
-   factors that appear to calm or aggravate symptoms
-   beliefs, fear, stress, sleep, and daily demands
-   pain science that may be relevant to the person's circumstances
-   realistic questions or next steps to discuss with the wider healthcare team

This does not mean pain is imagined or caused by a single factor. It also does not establish that pain is neuroplastic. A careful service should be clear about those boundaries.

## Questions to Ask Before Booking

Before choosing any chronic pain service, consider asking:

1. What professional role and scope does the provider work within?
2. What training is relevant to the approach being offered?
3. Is the service educational, therapeutic, clinical, or a combination?
4. What medical assessment should take place first?
5. What happens during a session, and what does it cost?
6. How will the provider respond if symptoms change or raise a medical concern?
7. Will the service work alongside, rather than replace, existing healthcare?

I have a public listing in the ATNS Practitioner & Coach Directory. A directory listing is useful background information, but you should still ask me directly to confirm the training and scope relevant to your needs.
