## Understanding Chronic Migraine vs Regular Migraines

Not all migraines are the same. A regular migraine might hit you a few times a month—debilitating when it happens, but with pain-free days in between. Chronic migraine is different. You're dealing with headaches on at least 15 days per month, with eight or more having migraine features like one-sided throbbing pain, nausea, or sensitivity to light and sound.

According to the Migraine Association of Ireland, migraine combined with medication overuse headache became the second largest cause of disability in Ireland in 2016. This isn't about being weak or not trying hard enough—it's about your nervous system becoming stuck in a hypersensitive state.

Common symptoms during chronic migraine attacks include:

-   Moderate to severe throbbing pain, usually one-sided
-   Nausea and vomiting
-   Extreme sensitivity to light (photophobia)
-   Sensitivity to sound (phonophobia)
-   Visual disturbances or aura (in some cases)
-   Difficulty concentrating or "brain fog"
-   Neck pain and stiffness
-   Fatigue that persists even after the headache resolves

Many people we work with in Ireland describe living with migraines as like having their life on hold—cancelled plans, missed work, relationships strained by unpredictability.

## Why Traditional Migraine Management Falls Short

If you've been managing migraines for years, you've probably tried the standard treatment pathway: over-the-counter painkillers, then triptans, then preventative medications like propranolol or topiramate. Some people progress to nerve blocks or even Botox injections.

These treatments can help, but here's the problem: they don't address why your nervous system has become so sensitised in the first place. And for many people, relying heavily on acute medications creates a vicious cycle.

**The Medication Overuse Headache Trap**

Research shows that more than two-thirds of medication overuse headaches occur in people with migraine. Taking acute pain medication more than 10-15 days per month can actually make migraines worse and more frequent. Your brain starts expecting the medication, and when it wears off, it triggers another headache.

It's not your fault—you're just trying to function. But there's a better way.

## A Different Approach to Chronic Migraine Treatment Ireland

Modern pain science has revealed something crucial about chronic migraine: while it has neurological triggers, the persistence of chronic pain involves your central nervous system becoming hypersensitive. This is where migraine pain relief strategies that retrain your nervous system become essential.

**Pain Neuroscience Education for Migraines**

Understanding how migraines work changes everything. When you learn that your migraine isn't caused by a "broken" brain but by a protective—if overzealous—nervous system response, it reduces fear and anxiety. That matters because stress is one of the most common migraine triggers, reported by about 70% of people with migraines.

We explain concepts like central sensitisation, where your nervous system's threat detection has become too sensitive. It's like a smoke alarm going off when you're just cooking toast—the alarm works, it's just overly reactive.

**Identifying and Managing Migraine Triggers**

Living with migraines often means living in fear of the next attack. Trigger factors are involved in about 40% of migraine attacks according to Irish migraine resources. Common triggers include:

-   Stress and anxiety
-   Poor or disrupted sleep
-   Skipping meals or dehydration
-   Hormonal changes (particularly in women)
-   Caffeine (too much or withdrawal)
-   Bright lights or strong odours
-   Neck tension or poor posture
-   Weather changes
-   Certain foods (though this varies greatly between individuals)

Here's the thing: you can't avoid all triggers all the time. That would create its own stress. Instead, we teach you how to build resilience so your nervous system doesn't react so strongly to unavoidable triggers.

**Movement and Lifestyle Modifications**

Research indicates that regular cardiovascular exercise—40 minutes, three times weekly—can be as effective as medication in reducing migraine frequency and severity. But many people avoid exercise because they fear it will trigger an attack.

Our migraine management approach includes graduated movement therapy, starting at your current level and building slowly. We also address sleep quality, stress management, and pacing strategies—all crucial factors in migraine pain relief.

**Breaking Free From Medication Dependence**

If you're experiencing medication overuse headaches, recovery typically involves carefully weaning off acute medications under medical supervision while building other pain recovery techniques. This isn't easy, but it can be transformative. People often find that once they break the overuse cycle and implement nervous system retraining, their baseline migraine frequency drops significantly.

## Common Questions About Living With Migraines

**Can chronic migraine really improve without more medication?**  
Yes. Many people reduce both migraine frequency and their reliance on acute medications by addressing nervous system sensitisation. This doesn't mean medication is bad—triptans and preventatives can be helpful—but they work better when combined with approaches that address the underlying hypersensitivity.

**What if I've already tried everything?**  
Most people we see have tried multiple medications and treatments. But if you haven't addressed the pain neuroscience education and nervous system retraining aspects, you haven't tried everything. This approach is fundamentally different from traditional migraine management.

**Will I need to avoid all my triggers forever?**  
No. The goal is building resilience so that occasional exposure to triggers doesn't automatically mean a migraine. Some people find they can reintroduce foods or activities they'd previously avoided once their nervous system becomes less reactive.

**How long does recovery take?**  
Everyone's different, but most people notice meaningful changes within 8-12 weeks. Full recovery—meaning migraine frequency is significantly reduced and impact on life is minimal—typically takes 3-6 months of consistent work.

## What Treatment Is Available in Ireland Through the HSE

It's worth knowing what's available through the public health system in Ireland. The HSE offers various chronic migraine treatment Ireland options, including:

-   GP-prescribed acute medications (triptans, anti-emetics, painkillers)
-   Preventative medications (propranolol, topiramate, amitriptyline, candesartan)
-   Referral to neurology specialists at Dublin and Cork hospitals
-   Access to CGRP inhibitors like erenumab (Aimovig) and fremanezumab (Ajovy) for eligible patients who've failed three preventative treatments

However, waiting lists can be long, and the focus remains primarily medical. Our approach complements HSE care by addressing the nervous system retraining that medications alone can't provide.
